10/F Auditorium of Ma Tau Kok Flatiron

Ma Tau Kok Flatiron

Office Tower in a Redeveloping District

01-05/2021

Bacherlor's Final Project

Situated at the edge of the urban fabric, the tower stands at the entrance of the historical and dense Ma Tau Kok district facing the new redeveloping Kai Tak. From a distance, the Ma Tau Kok flatiron and its accompanying smaller twin frames the rest of the To Kwa Wan skyline behind. Historically, Ma Tau Kok and surrounding areas were subject to a building height restriction due to the nearby Kai Tak Airport until its closure in 1988. No longer subject to height restrictions, soaring "pencil towers" have begun sprouting up in this evolving district.

While the Ma Tau Kok Flatiron embraces change and attention as a proposed vibrant, new landmark, its form and design is intended to be a positive contrast to the looming presences of other new tower developments. On the street level, the dominant line of sight follows the main Ma Tau Chung Road, such that the slender flat-iron form, placed parallel, greatly minimizes the volumetric presence of this 200m high building. Its slender form is further emphasized by the cross-bracing and colourful facade articulation. At the corners of the flatiron, colour and vertical fins density becomes gradually lighter and increase with density to create an illusion and elongate the form. Blue coloured vertical fins are scattered increasingly more the higher up the floors. Therefore, the tower appears to dissolve into the sky.

MAIN TOWER TYPICAL PLAN

Office working spaces are typically arranged along the long east and west facades. Due to its flatiron form, the unconventional corner spaces serve as social and public spaces such as breakout spaces, and meeting rooms.

MAIN TOWER PUBLIC FLOOR PLAN (49/F)

With a triangular plan, two distinct cores are created. The main, large, "private" core serves the office workers of the tower while a small "public" core brings visitors to open floors.

TOWER II TYPICAL PLAN

Due to its smaller footprint and lower height, one main core can sufficiently serve the entire tower. Socialising spaces are arranged along the western facade, which faces back into the central plaza of the development.

FACADE PATTERN STUDIES

By creating these 1:50 facade concept models, I was able to study variations in spandrel gradient, louver density, louver colours, louver width, and how these varied factors achieve different visual effects. Constructing them on a tracing paper base also allowed it to be fully malleable and explore the effects under different curvatures.
